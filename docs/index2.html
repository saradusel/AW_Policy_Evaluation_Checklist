Checklist for Animal Welfare in Policy Evaluations

The checklist covers the non-anthropocentric monetisation in CBA, and inclusion in SWF, of impacts on animals. It is based on a critical literature review.

The Checklist

1. How should welfare be measured?

1.1. Which fundamental concept of welfare should be adopted?

Experientialism / Hedonism
Welfare is determined by what the individual experiences.
Most common form: hedonism (feelings of pleasure or harm/suffering determine welfare).		 Preferentialism
Fulfilment of the individual‚Äôs own preferences determines welfare.		 Objective-good concept
Availability of certain goods determines welfare.		 Not specified
Approach is compatible with all concepts of welfare.

Hedonism is adopted in the most advanced studies in the field, those that include a parameterised example: Espinosa and Treich (2024a); Espinosa and Treich (2024b); Espinosa (2023); Kuruc and McFadden (2023); Fischer (2023).
Besides, general framework for hedonism: Budolfson et al. (2024)
Criticism:
Fleurbaey and Leppanen (2021) criticise hedonism for being ‚Äúpartly anthropocentric‚Äù (p.271) because mental states matter a lot to humans, but this may not be true for other species. Hence, humans receive implicit priority over other species (ibid.).
On the contrary, Fischer (2022) argues that it would not make much of a difference to adopt an objective-good concept instead of hedonism because, even if other goods were included, hedonic goods (especially, pain!) would be most important by far for all species.
Theoretical frameworks for preference-based non-anthropocentric CBA or SWF: Budolfson et al. (2024), Fleurbaey and Leppanen (2021), Lusk and Norwood (2012), Marggraf et al. (2012). These frameworks are rather basic and not yet connected to actual findings from animal experiments.
Criticism:
Browning (2023) finds preference-based approaches ‚Äúfar less convincing for animals‚Äù (p.535) than for humans, mainly because she suspects self-harming preferences in animals that, unlike for humans (e.g., smoking), could not be accounted for by defining a set of idealised rational preferences.
However, Browning (2023) does not provide convincing reasons why this should not be possible, she just posits that she cannot imagine idealised rational preferences could be defined for animals.
Although Fleurbaey and Leppanen (2021) acknowledge the existence of problematic preferences in animals (as in humans), they consider it possible to account for them, only this work has not been done for animals yet.
Vi≈°ak (2022) has critically reviewed the objective-good approaches by Kagan (2019) and McMahan (1996). She points out that these approaches may have very different implications depending on whether goods are included that animals and humans can attain to the same extent (e.g., health), or not (e.g., playing the piano).
In this context, Vi≈°ak (2022) criticises the lack of commitment in the literature to any specific list of goods.
Further, Vi≈°ak (2022) criticises that many of the proposed goods are more plausibly instrumental for experiencing pleasure (i.e., for hedonism) than that they are ends in themselves.

Fleurbaey and Leppanen (2021) see the main advantage of the objective-good concept in its flexibility to account for differences between species (as compared to hedonism). They propose to apply weights to the goods depending on how important these are for different species. In their view, such weights should be informed by species‚Äô preferences. In turn, rather than using preference-based weights for the objective-good concept, Fleurbaey and Leppanen (2021) suggest to adopt preferentialism altogether.

Espinosa and Treich (2024a) adopt a ‚Äúhybrid approach mixing the hedonistic and objective list approach‚Äù (p.19). However, we do not consider this theoretically consistent because we take the welfare concepts as mutually exclusive. Instead, based on Vi≈°ak (2022), we would characterise their approach as hedonism, using resource-based technical indicators to infer hedonic welfare states.
Study with parameterised example: Rusman et al. (2023)

General frameworks: Espinosa and Treich (2024c); Eichner and Runkel (2022); Zuber et al. (2022); Budolfson and Spears (2020); Johansson-Stenman (2018); Blackorby and Donaldson (1992).

1.2. Does the capacity for welfare differ between species?

Yes, different capacity
The minimum and maximum attainable level of welfare differs between species.		 No, equal capacity
The minimum and maximum attainable level of welfare is the same across species.		 Not specified
Approach is compatible with different or equal welfare capacity.

Presuming hedonism, differences in the welfare capacity between species are justified with the biological underpinnings of pleasure and suffering in Budolfson et al. (2024), Espinosa and Treich (2024a); Espinosa and Treich (2024b); Espinosa (2023); Fischer (2023); Gaffney et al. (2023); Schukraft (2020a); Budolfson and Spears (2020).
This may be related to differences in cognitive capacities (e.g., mental time travel), which may be positively or negatively correlated with welfare capacities, and/or to differences in emotional complexity (e.g., romantic love) (Fischer 2023; Schukraft 2020a).
Fischer (2023) checks one-by-one which of these cognitive or emotional features are present in different species and derives welfare capacities from this, while Espinosa and Treich (2024a); Espinosa and Treich (2024b); Espinosa (2023); Rusman et al. (2023), and Budolfson and Spears (2020) rely on the number of cortical neurons to estimate welfare capacities.

Fleurbaey and Leppanen (2021) assume different capacities within an objective-good or preferentialist concept of welfare.
Criticism:
The number of cortical neurons as a basis for welfare capacities is strongly criticised (reviewed by Shriver 2022), and even those who apply it acknowledge this.
In the complex list by Fischer (2023), it can be criticised, based on Vi≈°ak (2022) and Fleurbaey and Leppanen (2021), that some of the items are irrelevant for experiences of pleasure and harm across species (e.g., why should solitary animals suffer because they do not engage in romantic love?). In fact, Fischer (2023) himself notes that his list might only show the variety of valenced conditions instead of the intensity, and leaves this for further research.
The shared evolutionary history across species, and in connection to this, the function of hedonicity to (de)motivate behaviour are generally used to justify equal capacities across species (Browning 2023; Vi≈°ak 2022).

If different welfare capacities are assumed, it is possible to either normalise measured welfare to the species-specific minimum and maximum levels, or to compare absolute values across species.
In the literature, different terminology exists to describe normalisation: Fleurbaey and Leppanen (2021) call it the ‚Äúmaximum principle‚Äù (p.279), Gaffney et al. (2023) describe it as ‚Äúspecies-relativized welfare impacts‚Äù (p.5), and Browning (2023) refers to normalisation as the ‚Äú[s]imilarity in capacity assumption‚Äù (p.537). The latter wording seems to be counterintuitive, but it encapsulates what Fleurbaey and Leppanen (2021) criticise about normalisation: normalisation wipes out differences between species because, even if absolute welfare levels differ vastly, two individuals may still be considered equally well-off if species-normalised values are used for comparison.
Vi≈°ak (2022) and Horta (2010) review specific methods for normalisation that differ with regards to which minimum and maximum values exactly are used (e.g., McMahan‚Äôs (1996) native potential account).

The following studies normalise hypothetical measured welfare levels to welfare capacities: Budolfson et al. (2024), Espinosa and Treich (2024a); Espinosa and Treich (2024b); Espinosa (2023); Fischer (2023); Gaffney et al. (2023).
Presuming hedonism, Browning (2023) and Vi≈°ak (2022) consider equal capacities as the main view in animal science, grounded in structural and functional similarities of pleasure and pain/suffering.

For preferentialism, Vi≈°ak (2022) argues on a conceptual level that this must be connected to equal capacities because welfare is determined solely by whether preferences are fulfilled, which has the same meaning across species.
This stands in contrast to Fleurbaey and Leppanen (2021) who combine preferentialism and different capacities based on preferences over different functionings, assuming that individuals are indifferent about functionings they cannot attain due to their species-membership.

For the objective-good concept of welfare, Vi≈°ak (2022) points out that certain goods (e.g., physical health) may be attained by animals and humans to the same extent, which would lead to the assumption of equal capacities.
Espinosa and Treich (2024c); Kuruc and McFadden (2023); Eichner and Runkel (2022); Zuber et al. (2022); Johansson-Stenman (2018); Lusk and Norwood (2012); Marggraf et al. (2012); Blackorby and Donaldson (1992).

1.3. Which indicators should be used to assess welfare?

Resource- or management-based indicators Animal-based indicators: preferences Animal-based indicators: other

Eichner and Runkel (2022): Inverse of stocking density (approximated by units of land input per animal)

Rusman et al. (2023) based on Scherer et al. (2018): Number of days on pasture (cattle), stocking density (broilers, laying hens, pigs), transport time (cattle, broilers, laying hens, pigs)

Five Freedoms (degree of violation according to expert assessment): Espinosa and Treich (2024a); Espinosa and Treich (2024b); Espinosa (2023)
Willingness to turn down feed for other resources (only general framework, no actual findings from animal experiments): Lusk and Norwood (2012), Marggraf et al. (2012), Blackorby and Donaldson (1992).
The five-freedoms expert assessment approach by Espinosa and Treich (2024a); Espinosa and Treich (2024b), and Espinosa (2023) implicitly includes animal-based information.
Other than that, the classical animal-based indicators (e.g., lameness) have not yet been introduced into the economic frameworks for non-anthropocentric monetisation in CBA, or inclusion in SWF, of impacts on animals.
This is a severe drawback. Yet, in theory, all approaches would be compatible with animal-based indicators, just this has not been done yet.

Of note, the complex indicator list by Fischer (2023) serves to estimate welfare capacities, not actual welfare at a specific point in time. Welfare capacities could be combined with resource-, management- or animal-based assessments of actual welfare.

2. How should the welfare of individuals be aggregated to determine which policy is optimal for society?

2.1. Should weights be used to give different ethical values to the species?

No, differences between species are already accounted for in the measurement steps (part 1) Yes, weights should be applied based on empirical reasoning
Weights can be applied independently of the assumptions on equal or different welfare capacities (part 1).		 Yes, weights should be applied based ethical deliberation
Weights can be applied independently of the assumptions on equal or different welfare capacities (part 1).

It is passionately debated whether possible differences between the species should be accounted for in the measurement steps and/or whether additional weights should be used during aggregation (Vi≈°ak 2022; Schukraft 2020b; Kagan 2019; Horta 2018; Horta 2017; Horta 2010).
An important limitation of the reviewed literature is that none of the studies actually measure welfare across species, although methods have been proposed by Browning (2023) and Gaffney et al. (2023).
Instead, at best, expert assessments as in Espinosa and Treich (2024a) are used to assess welfare states but by design, these fail to capture any differences between species. Hence, to date, all studies in the field rely on additional weights.
Weights based on the same empirical reasoning as for welfare capacities (step 1.2) are applied by Budolfson et al. (2024); Espinosa and Treich (2024a); Espinosa and Treich (2024b); Espinosa (2023).
In fact, in these studies, the same weights are applied twice: First, to infer differences in welfare capacities between species. Then, welfare levels are normalised to the different capacities, which wipes out any differences between species (see part 1.2).
But because the authors want to hold on to these differences, they apply the same weights again in the aggregation step. The authors themselves do not comment on this.

In the studies by Rusman et al. (2023) and Scherer et al. (2018), weights are only applied in the aggregation step.
Criticism:
An important criticism of applying weights during aggregation is that these weights can overrule anything from the previous steps, which means that equal interests of individuals from different species do not receive equal consideration (Horta 2018; Horta 2017; Horta 2016).
Espinosa and Treich (2024a) realise this themselves and mention that the same pain would be valued differently in their approach, depending on whether it is experienced by a pig or a human. They propose to apply equal weights to equal interests, but this is only a quick fix for a problem that arises from their normalisation approach (see part 1.2).
In contrast, Kagan (2019) does not find it problematic to use weights during aggregation because in fact, if no weights are applied during aggregation but different capacities are inferred from species weights, this leads to the same results (also noted by Schukraft 2020b).
Budolfson et al. (2024); Espinosa and Treich (2024a); Espinosa and Treich (2024b); Espinosa (2023), and Kuruc and McFadden (2023) introduce an additional weight in their approaches to titrate the level of non-anthropocentrism in the analyses according to societal expectations.
